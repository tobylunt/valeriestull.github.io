    Valerie Stull is an environmental social scientist and global health professional who integrates multiple frameworks and disciplines to elucidate the linkages between agriculture, the environment, and human health. Her interdisciplinary work explores ways to ameliorate current constraints on food security and food sovereignty by evaluating the social, environmental, and health implications of innovative agricultural initiatives and development efforts. Currently, Valerie is investigating farmed edible insects as a sustainable protein source in Zambia and the United States.

    As a former international development professional and Fulbright researcher, Valerie is passionate about finding ways to optimize the use of edible insects for human nutrition, smart economic development, and as a means to improve planetary health. She is the Co-Founder of MIGHTi (the Mission to Improve Global Health Through Insects), a collaborative research project that is currently investigating social perceptions of edible insects in contexts where entomophagy is practiced, the impact of edible cricket consumption on the human gut microbiome, how supplementing protein-deficient mice with cricket powder affects metabolic and immune function biomarkers, as well as the nutritional value of insects reared using agricultural byproducts. Outside of academia, Valerie is a poet, outdoor enthusiast, and social justice advocate. She is currently seeking employment in the global health sector.
